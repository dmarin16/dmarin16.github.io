Even though we have explored some of the negatives of being an international student, not everything is bad. When studying abroad there's many things that help international students feel proud and connected to their new home. There are also many strategies that help mitigate the mental health issues international students suffer from. One strategy that has increased in popularity is mindfulness. There has been an increase in studies advocating for the use of mindfulness in universities. Altinyelken (2022) identifies the positive aspects of mindfulness by stating that “In addition, the inclusion of mindfulness in university settings can help students to gain adaptive coping strategies and awareness of their mind-body connection.” These practices are easy to use and understand, and free mindfulness apps make these practices accessible to everyone. Champion, Economides, and Chandler (2018) studied the effects of mindfulness on adults; the study found that mindfulness improves resilience and lowers stress levels when using free apps like headspace. Prasath et al. (2022) find that optimism, resilience, and self-efficacy play a crucial protective role in mitigating distress. Prasath et al. show how Psychological Capital can help international people mitigate those distress feelings. They explain Psychological Capital as hope, optimism, resilience and efficiency. Then they detail each one as “enables persevering toward goals and redirecting paths when necessary (hope), making a positive attribution about now and the future (optimism), sustaining and bouncing back and even beyond when beset by problems and adversity (resilience), and having the confidence to engage in the necessary effort to succeed at challenging tasks (efficacy)”. Prasath et al argue that if international students use those Psychological Capital resources they could feel better and improve their mental health. Tan et al (2025) show how a strong social support helps lower the chances of depression and anxiety amongst international students. They argue that the most effective ways to get social support “include contacts with direct family, secondary family, old friends in the home country, new friends in the host country, churches, school organisations, campus international student centers and community activities”. If international students manage to find a community where they feel comfortable, safe and heard, they are less likely to show signs of distress. 

Why international students choose Canadian institutions is also important when discussing the positive aspects of being an international student. The Canadian Bureau for International Education acknowledges some reasons in their international student survey (2024) and amongst the top ones are: the ability to work and study at the same time as that helps them gain relevant work experience, the opportunity for permanent residency, safety and stability, and the quality educational system Canada offers. Many international students are looking to improve their quality of life and using that as motivation can lead to them feeling better about leaving their home so they can have a better future overall.  

Finally, one overlooked tool that helps many international students is having a good self-care routine. Wiltshire (2025) identifies steps for a good self-care routine and he emphasizes the importance of exercising. He explains how our physical health is connected to our mental health and that exercising will make it easier to improve both at the same time. He also shows how important digital cleansing can benefit mental health. Many students use their phones before bed and that can lead to bad sleep and that affects our body and mind which makes it harder to complete tasks and be productive.  Other strategies also include eating healthy, nature walks, and reading. These suggestions are from an international student that has used many of these tools to improve their mental health and help others succeed in their journey.
